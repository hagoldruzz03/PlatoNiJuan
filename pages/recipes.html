Adobong Manok

Filipino Chicken Adobo Recipe

Chicken Adobo is an authentic Filipino dish and is one of the mostly recognized Filipino foods. Not to be mistaken with Mexican adobo, this dish is uniquely prepared by stewing chicken in vinegar and soy sauce. Several sources who are experts in Asian food history say that the Filipinos were already cooking adobo even before Spanish colonization. According to them, cooking with vinegar preserves the meat — this method is also considered as one of the earliest food preservation practices. The taste of juicy chicken in a succulent, umami adobo sauce makes chicken adobo a comfort food for all ages. From kids to adults, we can't get enough of this mouthwatering meal!

Prep20 mins
Cook45 mins
Total1 hr 5 mins
Servings6

Ingredients

  • 2 tablespoons vegetable oil
  • 1 (3 pound) chicken, cut into pieces
  • 1 large onion, quartered and sliced
  • 2 tablespoons minced garlic
  • ⅔ cup low sodium soy sauce
  • ⅓ cup white vinegar
  • 1 tablespoon garlic powder
  • 2 teaspoons black pepper
  • 1 bay leaf

Instructions

  1. Heat vegetable oil in a large skillet over medium-high heat. Cook chicken pieces until golden brown, 2 to 3 minutes per side. Transfer chicken to a plate and set aside.
  2. Add onion and garlic to the skillet; cook until softened and brown, about 6 minutes.
  3. Pour in soy sauce and vinegar and season with garlic powder, black pepper, and bay leaf.
  4. Return chicken to pan, increase heat to high, and bring to a boil. Reduce heat to medium-low, cover, and simmer until chicken is tender and cooked through, 35 to 40 minutes.

Afritadang Manok

Filipino Chicken Afritada Recipe

Chicken Afritada, or afritadang manok, is a type of Filipino chicken stew with a tomato-based sauce. It is a simple dish that can be cooked on regular days — perfect for lunch and better when eaten with warm white rice. The name afritada traces its roots to the Spanish cooking method of pan-frying (freír), which Filipino cooks adopted to first brown potatoes, carrots, vegetables, and chicken before letting everything stew in savory aromatics and tomato sauce. This improved version adds butter and green peas for extra richness.

Prep15 mins
Cook45 mins
Total1 hr
Servings6

Ingredients

  • 1 kg chicken (drumstick or wings), cut in serving sizes
  • 1 pc big potato, cut in cubes
  • 2 pcs carrots, cut in cubes
  • 1 onion, chopped
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 pack (250g) tomato sauce
  • 1 cup water
  • 1 small green bell pepper, cut in cubes
  • 1 small red bell pepper, cut in cubes
  • ½ cup green peas
  • 3 tbsp cooking oil
  • 3 tbsp butter
  • Salt and pepper, to taste

Instructions

  1. In a cooking pot, pour-in and heat the cooking oil in low to medium heat.
  2. Add-in the potato cubes and the carrot cubes and fry until lightly browned. Set aside.
  3. Add-in the chopped onion and minced garlic and sauté for 2 minutes or until aromatic and soft.
  4. Add-in the chicken and season with salt and pepper. Let it simmer for 5 to 10 minutes or until the chicken is nicely browned.
  5. Add-in the tomato sauce and water. Let it simmer in low heat for 20 to 40 minutes or until the chicken is fully cooked inside.
  6. Add-in the potato cubes and carrot cubes back, the bell pepper cubes, green peas and the butter. Stir well and cook for another 5 minutes.
  7. Transfer to a serving bowl. Serve and enjoy!

Chicken Barbeque

Filipino Chicken Barbeque Recipe

Good food often leaves quite the impression after even just the first taste — the aroma, the blend of flavors, and the ever-pleasant textures are not easy to forget. And Chicken Barbecue done right — with just about the perfect amount of savor and juiciness — possesses that same unforgettable quality. The barbecue method is believed to have ancient origins, recorded in a Spanish journal entry about Caribbean tribes who slow-cooked food on raised wooden platforms over hot coals. Today, this tradition lives on vibrantly in Filipino street food culture and backyard gatherings, where skewers of marinated chicken are grilled and basted continuously for a smoky, caramelized crust.

Prep12 hrs 10 mins
Cook30 mins
Total12 hrs 40 mins
Servings6

Ingredients

  • 3 pounds whole chicken, butterflied
  • 1/2 cup soy sauce
  • 1/4 cup calamansi or lemon juice
  • 1/2 cup brown sugar
  • 1 head garlic, peeled and minced
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 1 teaspoon pepper
  • 1/4 cup canola oil

Instructions

  1. Rinse chicken and pat dry.
  2. In a bowl, combine soy sauce, calamansi juice, brown sugar, garlic, salt, and pepper. Stir well until sugar and salt are dissolved.
  3. Add chicken and massage with the marinade until fully coated. Cover and marinate in the refrigerator for at least 2 hours or up to overnight. Drain chicken well, discarding marinade.
  4. In a wide, thick-bottomed pan over medium heat, heat oil. Add chicken and cook for about 15 to 20 minutes on each side or until nicely browned on the outside and a thermometer inserted in the center reads 165°F.
  5. Remove from heat and let stand for about 5 minutes before cutting into serving pieces. Serve hot.

Chicken Caldereta

Filipino Chicken Caldereta Recipe

Chicken Caldereta is a type of Filipino chicken stew simmered to perfect tenderness in a spicy tomato sauce with potatoes, bell peppers, and green olives. A good amount of liver spread is added to make the sauce more flavorful — this ingredient makes the dish unique in a very good way. Caldereta's name is derived from the Spanish word caldera meaning cauldron, and the dish was brought to the Philippines by the Spaniards during their 300-year occupation. It shares similarities with afritada and mechado and is a popular dish served during special occasions, parties, and festivities.

Prep15 mins
Cook45 mins
Total1 hr
Servings6

Ingredients

  • 1/2 cup pineapple juice
  • 1 (3 to 4 pounds) whole chicken, cut into serving parts
  • 1/4 cup soy sauce
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • 1/4 cup canola oil
  • 2 medium potatoes, peeled and quartered
  • 1 large carrot, peeled and cut diagonally into 1/2-inch thick
  • 1 small green bell pepper, cored, seeded and cut into 2-inch cubes
  • 1 small red bell pepper, cored, seeded and cut into 2-inch cubes
  • 1 onion, peeled and chopped
  • 3 cloves garlic, peeled and minced
  • 3 Thai chili peppers, stemmed and chopped
  • 2 cups tomato sauce
  • 1 1/2 cups water
  • 1/4 cup green olives
  • 1/2 cup liver spread
  • 1/2 cup Eden cheese or any quick melt cheese

Instructions

  1. In a large bowl, combine chicken, pineapple juice, soy sauce, and salt and pepper to taste. Marinate for about 30 minutes. Drain very well, reserving liquid.
  2. In a wide pan over medium heat, heat oil. Add potatoes and cook until lightly browned. Remove from pan and drain on paper towels.
  3. Add carrots and cook until lightly browned. Remove from pan and drain on paper towels.
  4. Add bell peppers and cook, stirring regularly, for about 30 seconds. Remove from pan and drain on paper towels.
  5. Add chicken and cook until lightly browned. Remove from pan and drain on paper towels. Discard excess oil, leaving about 2 tablespoons.
  6. Add onions and garlic and cook until softened.
  7. Add tomato sauce, water, and reserved marinade. Bring to a boil.
  8. Add chili peppers and cook for about 30 seconds. Add chicken and olives.
  9. Lower heat, cover, and continue to cook for about 15 to 20 minutes or until chicken is tender and cooked through.
  10. Add potatoes and carrots and cook until tender.
  11. Add liver spread and stir until well-distributed.
  12. Add cheese and stir until melted. Season with salt and pepper.
  13. Add bell peppers. Continue to cook for about 2 to 3 minutes or until sauce is thickened. Serve hot.

Chicken Curry

Filipino Chicken Curry Recipe

The Philippines is considered as the melting pot of Asia — people of different races and origins call it their home, and along with themselves they brought their culture, tradition, and food. This is the reason why spices such as curry made their debut in Philippine cuisine. This Pinoy Chicken Curry is the Filipino version of the popular dish: delicious, easy to cook, with a rich, creamy, and flavorful sauce that goes well with warm white rice. What makes it distinctly Filipino is the use of fish sauce, bell pepper, celery, and coconut milk — ingredients not found in traditional Indian versions.

Prep15 mins
Cook45 mins
Total1 hr
Servings6

Ingredients

  • 1/4 cup canola oil
  • 2 medium potatoes, peeled and quartered
  • 2 large carrots, peeled and cubed
  • 1/2 green bell pepper, cored, seeded and cut into cubes
  • 1/2 red bell pepper, cored, seeded and cut into cubes
  • 1 onion, peeled and cubed
  • 3 cloves garlic, peeled and minced
  • 1 thumb-size ginger, peeled and julienned
  • 1 (3 pounds) bone-in chicken, cut into serving pieces
  • 1 tablespoon fish sauce
  • 1 cup coconut milk
  • 1 cup water
  • 1 tablespoon curry powder
  • Salt and pepper to taste

Instructions

  1. In a wide pan over medium heat, heat oil. Add potatoes and cook for about 2 to 3 minutes or until lightly browned. Remove from pan and drain on paper towels.
  2. Add carrots and cook for about 1 to 2 minutes. Remove from pan and drain on paper towels.
  3. Remove excess oil from pan except for about 1 tablespoon. Add bell peppers and cook for about 30 to 40 seconds. Remove from pan and set aside.
  4. Add onions, garlic, and ginger and cook until softened.
  5. Add chicken and cook, stirring occasionally, until lightly browned.
  6. Add fish sauce and continue to cook for about 1 minute.
  7. Add coconut milk and water. Bring to a simmer, skimming any scum that may float on top.
  8. Lower heat, cover, and simmer for about 20 to 30 minutes or until chicken is cooked.
  9. Add potatoes and carrots and cook for about 3 to 5 minutes or until tender.
  10. Add curry powder and stir to combine. Continue to cook for about 2 to 3 minutes or until sauce starts to thicken.
  11. Season with salt and pepper to taste.
  12. Add bell peppers and cook for about 1 minute or until tender yet crisp. Serve hot.

Ginataang Manok

Filipino Chicken Ginataan Recipe

Ginataang Manok is translated as chicken stewed in coconut milk — a simple Filipino dish wherein chicken is sautéed in garlic, onion, and ginger then stewed in coconut milk. A more traditional approach requires native hen, usually raised in the backyard, which is much tastier compared to broiler chickens and adds more flavor to the dish. The dish is very similar to tinola or binakol, except for the use of coconut milk, which gives it a wonderfully rich, creamy character. It is one of the best representations of how Filipino cooking makes the most of the abundant coconut.

Prep10 mins
Cook40 mins
Total50 mins
Servings6

Ingredients

  • 1/4 cup canola oil
  • 2 medium potatoes, peeled and quartered
  • 1 onion, peeled and sliced thinly
  • 2 cloves garlic, peeled and minced
  • 1 thumb-size ginger, peeled and julienned
  • 1 (3 to 4 pounds) whole chicken, cut into parts
  • 1 tablespoon fish sauce
  • 1 can (13.5 ounces) coconut milk
  • 1/2 cup water
  • 4 Thai chili peppers, stemmed and chopped
  • 1/2 red bell pepper, seeded, cored and sliced into strips
  • 1/2 green bell pepper, seeded, cored and sliced into strips
  • Salt and pepper to taste

Instructions

  1. In a wide skillet over medium heat, heat oil. Add potatoes and cook, turning as needed, until lightly browned. With a slotted spoon, remove potatoes from the pan and drain on paper towels.
  2. Remove excess oil from pan except for about 2 tablespoons. Add onions, garlic, and ginger and cook until softened.
  3. Add chicken and cook until lightly browned.
  4. Add fish sauce and cook for about 1 minute.
  5. Add coconut milk, water, and chili peppers. Bring to a simmer, skimming any scum that floats on top.
  6. Lower heat, cover, and continue to cook for about 20 to 25 minutes or until chicken is cooked through and liquid is reduced.
  7. Add potatoes and cook for about 3 to 5 minutes or until potatoes are tender and sauce is thickened.
  8. Add bell peppers and cook for another 1 to 2 minutes or until tender yet crisp.
  9. Season with salt and pepper to taste. Serve hot.

Chicken Inasal

Filipino Chicken Inasal Recipe

Chicken Inasal is a grilled chicken dish and one of the well-known Ilonggo dishes — done by marinating chicken pieces in a unique blend of calamansi, palm vinegar, lemongrass, ginger, and brown sugar, then grilling over hot coals while basting with annatto-infused chicken oil. It is a popular specialty in the city of Bacolod, where an entire street market called "Manokan Country" is dedicated to it. Its origin can be traced back to Bacolod's Cuadra Street in the 1970s. Restaurant chains such as Mang Inasal and Bacolod Chicken Inasal have since brought this celebrated dish nationwide.

Prep20 mins
Cook30 mins
Total50 mins + marinating
Servings8

Ingredients

  • 2 stalks lemongrass
  • 1 head garlic, peeled and minced
  • 1 thumb-size ginger, peeled and grated
  • 1/2 cup palm vinegar
  • 1/4 cup calamansi juice
  • 1/4 cup brown sugar
  • 1 1/2 teaspoons salt
  • 1/2 teaspoon pepper
  • 2 (about 3 to 4 pounds each) whole chicken, quartered
  • For the Annatto Oil:
  • 1 pound chicken skins, bottoms, and/or fat
  • 1/4 cup annatto seeds
  • 2 garlic cloves, peeled and pounded
  • 1 bay leaf

Instructions

  1. Peel and/or cut away the dark, fibrous parts of the lemongrass until you get to the white and light green portions. Using a knife, finely chop lemongrass.
  2. In a large bowl, combine vinegar, calamansi juice, garlic, ginger, lemongrass, sugar, salt, and pepper. Whisk until well-blended. Add chicken, cover bowl, and marinate in the refrigerator for 2 to 4 hours.
  3. In a wide pan over medium heat, place chicken fat and cook, turning as needed, until crisp and renders oil. Using a slotted spoon, remove skin and bottoms.
  4. Lower heat and add annatto seeds, crushed garlic, and bay leaf. Cook for about 1 to 2 minutes. Remove from heat and allow the oil to steep for about 1 hour until infused with deep orange color. Using a fine-mesh sieve, strain oil and set aside.
  5. Lightly grease grill grates and heat over hot coals.
  6. Drain chicken from marinade and scrape off any stray aromatics. Arrange in a single layer over hot grates with skin side up.
  7. Grill chicken, turning once or twice and basting regularly with half the chicken oil, for about 30 to 40 minutes or until a thermometer inserted in the thickest part reads 160°F.
  8. Remove from heat, cover, and allow to rest for about 3 to 5 minutes. Serve hot with the remaining chicken oil and steamed rice.

Pininyahang Manok

Filipino Chicken Pininyahan Recipe

Pininyahang Manok literally translates to pineapple chicken. This recipe calls for chicken slices to be marinated in pineapple juice then stewed with pineapple chunks. Aside from pineapples, secondary ingredients such as carrots, bell pepper, and milk make up the dish. One favorite variation is Pininyahang Manok sa Gata, which uses coconut milk instead of evaporated or fresh milk — it is richer and equally delicious. This dish is both healthy and delicious, providing a good amount of protein and vitamins.

Prep15 mins
Cook40 mins
Total55 mins
Servings4

Ingredients

  • 1 tablespoon canola oil
  • 1 medium red bell pepper, seeded, cored and cut into chunks
  • 1 onion, peeled and thinly sliced
  • 3 cloves garlic, peeled and minced
  • 3 pounds chicken, cut into serving parts
  • 1 cup pineapple juice
  • 1 cup pineapple chunks
  • 1 cup table cream
  • Salt and pepper to taste

Instructions

  1. In a pot over medium heat, heat oil.
  2. Add onions and garlic and cook, stirring regularly, until softened.
  3. Add bell peppers and cook, stirring regularly, for about 30 seconds. Remove from heat and set aside.
  4. Add chicken and cook, stirring occasionally, until lightly browned.
  5. Add pineapple juice. Bring to a boil, skimming any scum that floats on top. Lower heat, cover, and simmer until chicken is tender, about 20 to 25 minutes.
  6. Add pineapple chunks and table cream. Season with salt and pepper to taste.
  7. Continue to simmer for about 3 to 5 minutes or until sauce is slightly thickened.
  8. Add bell peppers and continue to cook for about 30 seconds or until tender-crisp. Serve hot.

Tinolang Manok

Filipino Chicken Tinola Recipe

Chicken Tinola is a Filipino soup dish that involves cooking chicken pieces in a fragrant ginger broth. It is a popular cold-weather dish — warming, nourishing, and deeply satisfying. Tinola is traditionally cooked with wedges of unripe papaya and malunggay leaves, making it a wonderful source of nutrients. The secret in making a good tinola is to simmer the chicken for longer periods of time — this extracts the flavor from the chicken and makes it tender in the process. Best enjoyed with fish sauce as a dipping condiment and a cup of warm white rice.

Prep15 mins
Cook50 mins
Total1 hr 5 mins
Servings4

Ingredients

  • 1 tablespoon canola oil
  • 1 small onion, peeled and sliced thinly
  • 3 cloves garlic, peeled and minced
  • 2 thumb-sized fresh ginger
  • 1 (3 to 4 pounds) whole chicken, cut into serving pieces
  • 2 tablespoons fish sauce
  • 5 cups water
  • 1 small green papaya, pared, seeded and cut into 2-inch wedges
  • 1 bunch fresh spinach leaves, stems trimmed
  • Salt and pepper to taste

Instructions

  1. In a pot over medium heat, heat oil. Add onions, ginger, and garlic and cook until softened.
  2. Add chicken and cook, stirring occasionally, for about 5 to 7 minutes or until chicken starts to change color and juices run clear.
  3. Add fish sauce and cook, stirring occasionally, for about 1 to 2 minutes.
  4. Add water and bring to a boil, skimming scum that floats on top. Lower heat, cover, and simmer for about 30 to 35 minutes or until chicken is cooked through.
  5. Add papaya and cook for about 3 to 5 minutes or until tender yet crisp.
  6. Season with salt and pepper to taste.
  7. Add spinach and push down into broth. Cook until just wilted. Serve hot.

Sweet & Sour Chicken

Filipino Chicken Sweet and Sour Recipe

This scrumptious recipe is one of the most satisfying stir-fries you can make at home. The chicken pieces are pan-fried — no deep frying required — giving them a nice mellow crunch while remaining moist and tender inside. The sauce is full of flavor and is not overly sweet, which is perfect for bringing out the natural sweetness of the onions and peppers. This one-skillet Sweet and Sour Chicken brings crunchy pan-fried chicken together with sweet bell peppers and pineapple chunks in a lightly sweetened sauce — it tastes so much better than takeout and comes together in about thirty minutes.

Prep30 mins
Cook20 mins
Total50 mins
Servings8

Ingredients

  • 1¾ cups water, divided
  • 1 (8 oz) can pineapple chunks, drained (juice reserved)
  • ¾ cup white sugar
  • ½ cup distilled white vinegar
  • 2 drops orange food color
  • ¼ cup cornstarch
  • 2¼ cups self-rising flour
  • 2 tablespoons vegetable oil
  • 2 tablespoons cornstarch
  • 1 egg
  • ½ teaspoon salt
  • ¼ teaspoon ground white pepper
  • 1½ cups water
  • 8 skinless, boneless chicken breast halves, cut into 1-inch cubes
  • 1 quart vegetable oil for frying
  • 2 green bell peppers, cut into 1-inch pieces

Instructions

  1. Combine 1½ cups of water, reserved pineapple juice, sugar, vinegar, and orange food coloring in a medium saucepan. Bring to a boil over medium heat; set aside.
  2. Mix ¼ cup cornstarch and ¼ cup water together until smooth; pour into the sauce, stirring continuously, until slightly thickened.
  3. Place flour, 2 tablespoons oil, 2 tablespoons cornstarch, egg, salt, and white pepper in a large bowl; gradually whisk in 1½ cups water to make a thick batter.
  4. Add chicken pieces; stir until well coated.
  5. Heat oil in a large, deep skillet or wok to 360°F (180°C). Fry chicken pieces until golden, about 10 minutes; remove and drain on paper towels.
  6. Layer green peppers, pineapple chunks, and cooked chicken pieces on a platter; pour hot sweet and sour sauce over top.

Sisig

Filipino Pork Sisig Recipe

Pork Sisig is a popular Filipino dish that can be considered as a main dish or an appetizer. The composition of this dish is mainly of pig's parts such as minced pork meat, ears, and face, combined with chicken liver, onions, and chili peppers. Sisig originated from Pampanga, considered as the culinary capital of the Philippines, and was invented by Lucia Cunanan — popularly known as Aling Lucing, the "Sisig Queen." The US military bases located in the area might have played a role in the invention of this dish, as cheap pig heads were available from commissaries. Sisig is synonymous with pulutan — the perfect pairing for beer or any alcoholic beverage.

Prep15 mins
Cook1 hr 30 mins
Total1 hr 45 mins
Servings6

Ingredients

  • 2 pounds pig face (snouts, ears, and jowls)
  • 1 cup vinegar
  • 1/4 cup soy sauce
  • 1 head garlic, peeled and crushed
  • 1 teaspoon whole peppercorns
  • 2 bay leaves
  • 1 tablespoon salt
  • Water
  • 1 large onion, peeled and diced
  • 6 Thai chili peppers, stemmed and minced
  • 1/2 cup calamansi juice
  • 1 tablespoon liquid seasoning (Maggi)
  • 1/2 cup liver spread
  • Pepper to taste

Instructions

  1. In a large pot, combine pig face, vinegar, soy sauce, garlic, peppercorns, bay leaves, about 1 tablespoon salt, and enough water to completely cover pork.
  2. Bring to a boil, skimming scum that floats on top. Lower heat, cover, and simmer for about 50 minutes to 1 hour or until meat is tender. Remove from heat and drain well, discarding liquid.
  3. Over a hot grill, grill pork for about 7 to 10 minutes on each side or until crisp and slightly charred. Or arrange in a single layer on a baking sheet and broil in a 450°F oven for about 4 to 5 minutes or until nicely charred.
  4. Remove from heat, allow to cool to touch, and chop meat.
  5. In a large bowl, combine chopped meat, onions, chili peppers, calamansi juice, and liquid seasoning.
  6. Add liver spread and gently stir to combine. Season with salt and pepper to taste.
  7. To serve, transfer to sizzling hot plates.

Bicol Express

Filipino Pork Bicol Express Recipe

The mild sweetness of coconut milk brings a sense of delight to the sharp notes of chili pepper and bagoong in the classic Bicol Express recipe. This contrast has long created a spell-binding richness in taste for the dish, making it arguably one of the most popular Filipino dishes. It contains a generous amount of pork belly enriched by shrimp paste and plenty of Thai and finger chilies. Bicol Express makes for an irresistible cold-day meal — with a creamy, delicious coconut milk stew and generous seasonings, the flavors come together beautifully. Pork is a fantastic source of protein, niacin, phosphorus, and vitamin B6.

Prep10 mins
Cook1 hr
Total1 hr 10 mins
Servings6

Ingredients

  • 1 tablespoon canola oil
  • 1 onion, peeled and sliced thinly
  • 4 cloves garlic, peeled and minced
  • 2 pounds pork belly, cut into 1-inch cubes
  • 2 tablespoons fresh shrimp paste
  • 1 can (13.5 ounces) coconut milk
  • 1 cup water
  • 14 pieces Thai chili peppers, stemmed and minced
  • 2 cups finger chilies (siling haba), sliced
  • 1 cup coconut cream
  • Salt and pepper to taste

Instructions

  1. In a wide pot over medium heat, heat oil. Add onions and garlic and cook, stirring occasionally, until softened.
  2. Add pork and cook, stirring occasionally, until lightly browned.
  3. Add shrimp paste and cook, stirring occasionally, for about 1 to 2 minutes.
  4. Add coconut milk, water, and chili peppers. Bring to a simmer and cook for about 35 minutes or until pork is tender and liquid is reduced and begins to render fat.
  5. Add finger chilies and cook, stirring regularly, for about 1 to 2 minutes.
  6. Add coconut cream and continue to simmer until thickened and begins to render fat.
  7. Season with salt and pepper to taste. Serve hot.

Pork Bistek

Filipino Pork Bistek Recipe

Pork Bistek is a Filipino twist on the classic Bistek Tagalog, using succulent pork chops as its meat. If you've already gotten a taste of Bistek, you would be aware of its unmistakably delicious saltiness and mild tang. These flavors go incredibly well with pork chops, which are more affordable and milder in taste than beef, giving the tasty soy-calamansi seasonings more of a chance to shine. The steps are quick and simple, making it a fantastic starter dish for a beginner cook. Pork chops are marinated, pan-fried, then braised until fork-tender and finished with caramelized onion rings.

Prep45 mins
Cook1 hr
Total1 hr 45 mins
Servings4

Ingredients

  • 3 pounds bone-in pork chops, about 1/4 inch thick
  • 1/2 cup calamansi or lemon juice
  • 1/4 cup soy sauce
  • 1 large onion, peeled and sliced thinly
  • 1 head garlic, peeled and minced
  • 1/4 teaspoon pepper
  • 3 tablespoons canola oil
  • 1 cup water
  • Salt to taste
  • 1 small onion, peeled and sliced into rings

Instructions

  1. In a bowl, combine pork, calamansi or lemon juice, soy sauce, onions, garlic, and pepper. Massage the marinade into the meat and marinate for about 30 minutes.
  2. Drain meat, onions, and garlic from marinade, squeezing and reserving excess liquid. Set aside.
  3. In a pan over medium-high heat, heat 2 tablespoons of the oil. Add pork chops and cook for about 2 to 4 minutes per side or until lightly browned. Spoon out and reserve released meat juices during frying. Remove meat from pan and keep warm.
  4. In the pan, add the remaining 1 tablespoon oil. Add the reserved onions and garlic, and cook, stirring regularly, until softened.
  5. Return meat to pan. Add reserved marinade and meat juices.
  6. Add water and bring to a boil, skimming scum that floats on top.
  7. Cover, lower heat, and simmer for about 40 to 50 minutes or until meat is fork-tender and liquid is reduced.
  8. Season with salt to taste. Add the onion rings. Turn off heat, cover, and allow onion slices to cook slightly in the steam. Serve hot.

Crispy Pata

Filipino Pork Crispy Pata Recipe

Crispy Pata or crispy pork leg is a popular Filipino pork dish that can be eaten as a main dish along with rice and atcharang papaya, or enjoyed as pulutan — best dipped in a spicy vinegar mixture. Cooking crispy pata is quite challenging, but everyone can do it with proper caution; it is a rewarding achievement for any home cook. The dish is similar to the German Schweinshaxe and consists of deep-fried pig trotters or knuckles: boiled until tender, then frozen overnight to achieve the crispiest skin, and finally deep-fried to golden perfection.

Prep8 hrs 10 mins
Cook2 hrs 20 mins
Total10 hrs 30 mins
Servings4

Ingredients

  • 1 whole pork leg
  • 1 cup vinegar
  • 1 can (12 ounces) 7-Up or Sprite
  • 1 head garlic, peeled and minced
  • 1 tablespoon peppercorns
  • 3 bay leaves
  • Salt
  • 5 cups oil
  • For the Dipping Sauce:
  • 1 cup vinegar
  • 1/4 cup soy sauce
  • 2 shallots, peeled and chopped
  • 3 cloves garlic, peeled and minced
  • 2 Thai chili peppers, minced
  • Salt and pepper to taste

Instructions

  1. With a knife, scrape any stray hairs on pork leg and trim off nails.
  2. In a large pot over medium heat, combine pork leg, vinegar, 7-Up, enough water to cover, garlic, peppercorns, bay leaves, and 1/3 cup salt. Bring to a boil, skimming scum that may accumulate on top.
  3. Lower heat, cover, and simmer for about 1½ to 2 hours or until meat is tender but not falling apart. Add more water in 1-cup increments if needed.
  4. Drain pork leg, discarding liquid and aromatics. Place on a rack and allow to cool to touch.
  5. Sprinkle with about 2 tablespoons salt and massage all over. Place in a container with a lid and freeze overnight.
  6. In a large, tall pot over medium heat, heat about 4 to 5 cups of oil to 350°F.
  7. Gently add pork leg and deep-fry, turning as needed, until golden and skin is crispy.
  8. Remove pork from oil and drain on a wire rack. Let rest for about 3 to 5 minutes before chopping. Serve hot with dipping sauce.
  9. Dipping Sauce: In a small bowl, combine vinegar, soy sauce, shallots, garlic, chili peppers, and salt and pepper to taste. Stir well.

Pork Dinuguan

Filipino Pork Dinuguan Recipe

Pork Dinuguan is a type of stew native to the Philippines — peculiar compared to other stews because of the use of pork blood as the main ingredient. It might seem different to those not yet familiar with it, but your impression can change in a very good way after trying it. This easy recipe is intended for beginner and intermediate cooks, using only pork belly with no innards involved. Dinuguan is a Filipino savory stew of pork simmered in a rich, spicy dark gravy of pig blood, garlic, chili, and vinegar. It is traditionally served hot with rice or paired with soft steamed puto.

Prep15 mins
Cook45 mins
Total1 hr
Servings6

Ingredients

  • 10 ounces pork blood
  • 1 cup vinegar
  • 1 tablespoon canola oil
  • 1 onion, peeled and chopped
  • 4 cloves garlic, peeled and minced
  • 1 thumb-size ginger (about 1 tablespoon), peeled and minced

Using pork in this recipe is optional; if you are one of our vegan friends, you can still enjoy this dish even without pork. Including pork adds a little meaty taste to it, but the string beans' natural flavor — often overpowered in dishes such as Pakbet or Sinigang — truly shines in this simple adobo preparation. Garnish with fried garlic bits for an extra touch.

Prep15 mins
Cook30 mins
Total45 mins
Servings4

Ingredients

  • 1 tablespoon canola oil
  • 1 pound pork belly, cut into thin strips (optional)
  • 1 onion, peeled and sliced thinly
  • 5 cloves garlic, peeled and minced
  • 3/4 cup vinegar
  • 1/4 cup soy sauce
  • 1/2 cup water
  • 1 bunch yard long beans (sitaw), ends trimmed and cut into 3-inch lengths
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • Fried garlic bits (optional)

Instructions

  1. In a skillet over medium heat, heat oil.
  2. Add onions and garlic and cook, stirring occasionally, until softened.
  3. Add pork and cook, stirring occasionally, until lightly browned.

  5. Add soy sauce and water. Cover, lower heat, and continue to cook until pork is cooked through and sauce is reduced.
  6. Remove the lid and increase the heat to medium. Add long beans and cook, stirring occasionally, for about 3 to 5 minutes or until tender-crisp.
  7. Transfer to a serving platter and garnish with fried garlic bits, if desired. Serve hot.

Filipino Chop Seuy Recipe

There are a great many stories about the origins of this dish. Laing

Filipino Laing Recipe

Laing is a Filipino dish composed of dried taro leaves and coconut milk, first introduced in the province of Bicol. It is one of the most famous Filipino dishes — popular within the Philippines and also internationally. Laing is a type of ginataan (Filipino dishes cooked in coconut milk) also referred to as ginataang laing. It is made of shredded or whole taro leaves with meat or seafood cooked in thick coconut milk spiced with labuyo chili, lemongrass, garlic, shallots, ginger, and shrimp paste. When you add the dried taro leaves, do not stir right away — let the leaves absorb the liquid; you will know enough liquid is absorbed when the leaves slowly sink and the color turns a bit darker.

Cook1 hr
Total1 hr 15 mins
Servings6

Ingredients

  • 2 ounces dried gabi (taro) leaves
  • 2 lemongrass stalks
  • 1 tablespoon canola oil

  • 4 cloves garlic, peeled and minced

  • 1 pound pork belly, diced
  • Pepper to taste
  • 1 tablespoon shrimp paste
  • 4 cups coconut milk
  • 8 Thai chili peppers, chopped

Monggo Guisado

Filipino Monggo Guisado Recipe

Making Monggo Guisado is easy. By soaking mung beans in water for several hours or overnight, you save a lot of cooking time — the beans absorb the water which helps soften their texture. The longer you cook the aromatics (garlic, onions, and tomatoes), the more flavor you develop through caramelization. Monggo Guisado is a healthy and delicious meal best topped with chicharon and paired with any fried dish like fish or liempo. Tip: if you're not a fan of ampalaya, use ampalaya leaves instead or just add more malunggay leaves.

Prep1 hr (includes soaking)
Cook30 mins
Total1 hr 30 mins
Servings4

Ingredients

  • 140 g whole mung beans
  • 1 L water
  • 30 g vegetable cooking oil
  • 40 g garlic cloves, mashed
  • 120 g medium-sized onion, chopped
  • 360 g medium-sized red tomatoes, chopped
  • 250 g pork strips
  • 2 pcs Knorr Pork Cube
  • 250 g ampalaya, sliced very thinly (optional)
  • 120 g malunggay leaves
  • Topping: ½ cup crushed pork chicharon (optional)

Instructions

  1. Wash the mung beans in water to get rid of unwanted dirt. Soak in water for a minimum of 1 hour — the longer you soak them, the less time you need to cook them. Remove any floating particles on top.
  2. Once beans are soft enough, sauté garlic, onions, and tomatoes in a saucepan over medium heat for about 2 minutes or more. The longer you cook these aromatics, the more flavor you develop through caramelization.
  3. Add the pork strips and sauté until nicely browned.
  4. Drain the beans from the soaking water and add into your pot. Pour in water and add the Knorr Pork Cubes. Simmer until the beans can easily be mashed. For a thinner soup consistency, add more water; for thicker, cook longer.
  5. Add the ampalaya and cook for 2 minutes more before adding the malunggay.
  6. Serve hot topped with chicharon and paired with any fried dish like fish or liempo.

Leche Flan

Filipino Style Leche Flan Recipe

Leche Flan is the ultimate sweet treat — a classic Filipino custard that is a guaranteed crowd-pleaser, rich, smooth, and creamy with a golden caramel topping. It is a classic Filipino dish adopted from the crème caramel of European origins. The difference is that Leche Flan does not use cream — it relies on evaporated milk and condensed milk for its creamy flavor, and the condensed milk adds a level of sweetness that makes it heavier and richer than crème caramel. It has been a regular menu item in restaurants worldwide because of its taste, ease of preparation, and long shelf life.

Prep15 mins
Cook1 hr
Total1 hr 15 mins
Servings12

Ingredients

  • 9 tablespoons sugar
  • 12 egg yolks, from large eggs
  • 1 can (14 ounces) sweetened condensed milk
  • 1 can (12 ounces) evaporated milk

Instructions

  1. Preheat oven to 375°F.
  2. Place 3 tablespoons of sugar in each of the three llaneras.
  3. Set llanera on the stove over low heat and using tongs, move repeatedly over flames until sugar is melted and turns into a golden liquid.
  4. Continuously tilt and swirl the llanera to ensure even melting and to distribute the liquid on the bottom of the mold. Remove from heat and allow caramel to cool and harden. Repeat with remaining llaneras.
  5. In a bowl, combine egg yolks and condensed milk. Whisk to combine.
  6. Add evaporated milk, gently stirring in a circular motion until blended.
  7. Using a cheesecloth or fine-mesh sieve, strain egg-milk mixture to remove stray egg whites.
  8. Pour mixture into prepared llaneras and cover tightly with foil. Arrange in a wide, oven-safe dish with about 1 inch of water (bain marie or water bath).
  9. Bake in the oven for about 50 minutes to 1 hour or until a toothpick inserted in the middle of custard comes out clean.
  10. Remove from oven, allow to cool, and refrigerate to chill and completely set. To serve, turn flan over on a serving plate, ending with caramel on top.

  5. Apply margarine on top, then sprinkle with shredded coconut.
  6. Serve with muscovado sugar.

Cheese Puto

Filipino Cheese Puto Recipe

Making your own Cheese Puto at home has never been easier — this recipe shows you how to make it from scratch in less than 40 minutes. It makes a wonderful snack and can even be eaten for breakfast with a hot cup of brewed coffee. Cheese Puto are soft, fluffy, and extra delicious with pockets of melted cheese. They are delicious on their own or served with dinuguan or pancit. This recipe can make up to 40 pieces and can be doubled or tripled depending on the number of pieces you intend to produce.

Prep20 mins
Cook10 mins
Total30 mins
Servings40 pcs

Ingredients

  • 1½ cups flour
  • 1 cup sugar
  • 1 tablespoon baking powder
  • 3 eggs
  • 6 ounces evaporated milk
  • 1/2 cup water
  • 2 tablespoons butter, melted
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • 1 cup cheddar cheese, shredded

Instructions

  1. In a large bowl, combine flour, sugar, and baking powder. Mix well.
  2. Add eggs one at a time and using an electric mixer, beat after each addition.
  3. Add milk and beat until smooth.
  4. Add water and continue to beat until batter is thin and smooth.
  5. Add butter and vanilla extract. Stir until well blended.
  6. Add 3/4 of the cheese and stir until combined.
  7. Fill molds with batter up to 3/4 full. Arrange molds in a single layer on steamer rack and place over steamer.
  8. Steam for about 8 to 10 minutes or until a toothpick inserted comes out clean.
  9. Turn off heat and top each puto with the remaining 1/4 cup of cheese. Cover for about 1 minute to allow residual heat to melt cheese.
  10. Allow to slightly cool before removing from molds.

Taho

Filipino Taho Recipe

Taho is a type of snack in the Philippines with a pudding-like texture — topped with a sweet brown syrup called arnibal and sago pearls. The taho vendor or "magtataho" can often be heard early in the morning selling taho on the streets. The white pudding part is actually soft silken tofu — made from scratch by the magtataho using soybeans, but also available in finished form from supermarkets. The brown syrup or arnibal is made of brown sugar, water, and optionally vanilla extract. This homemade recipe shows you how to recreate this beloved Filipino street food in your own kitchen.

Prep5 mins
Cook10 mins
Total15 mins
Servings8

Ingredients

  • 1 cup water
  • 1 cup dark brown sugar
  • 1 pandan leaf, tied into a knot
  • 1/2 cup sago pearls
  • 2 packages (12 ounces each) soft silken tofu

Instructions

  1. In a pot over medium heat, bring enough water to cover sago pearls to a boil. Add sago pearls, stir gently, and cook for about 10 minutes or until translucent. Remove from heat, rinse well, and drain.
  2. In the pot, add boiled sago and enough cold water to cover. Cook over medium heat, stirring occasionally, and bring to a gentle boil for about 6 to 10 minutes. Remove from heat, rinse well, and drain. Repeat the process until pearls are tender but chewy and translucent with no white in the center.
  3. Under cold running water, rinse sago a few times to remove excess starch. Allow to cool.
  4. In a saucepan over medium heat, add water and pandan leaf and bring to a boil for about 2 to 3 minutes. Remove and discard pandan leaf.
  5. Add brown sugar. Stir continuously until sugar is dissolved. Simmer, stirring continuously, until mixture is slightly thickened and syrupy.
  6. Add cooked sago and continue to cook for about 2 to 3 minutes.
  7. Place tofu block in a cheesecloth-lined steamer and steam for about 7 to 10 minutes or until heated through. (Alternatively, place in an oven-safe dish, loosely wrap with plastic film, and microwave for about 40 to 50 seconds.)
  8. Using a wide, flat serving spoon, divide tofu into serving bowls. Spoon sago and syrup on top. Serve warm.

Ube Halaya

Filipino Ube Halaya Recipe

From leche flan to halo-halo, the Philippines is anything but short of delicious local desserts to try. With its comfortingly velvety texture and mildly sweet taste, Ube Halaya is among the most deliciously well-balanced. Some people also call it ube jalea, ube jam, or ube haleya — but they all stand for the same consistently creamy and delicious dessert. Halayang Ube is a classic Filipino delicacy made of purple yam, coconut milk, butter, and cheese. Creamy, cheesy, and topped with golden latik, it is delicious as a snack or dessert and is also a key component in halo-halo and many other Filipino sweets.

Prep10 mins
Cook1 hr
Total1 hr 10 mins
Servings12

Ingredients

  • 1 package (16 ounces) frozen grated ube, thawed
  • 1 can (13.5 ounces) coconut milk
  • 1 can (14 ounces) sweetened condensed milk
  • 1 can (12 ounces) evaporated milk
  • 1/2 cup butter
  • 1 cup sugar
  • 2 to 3 drops ube extract (optional)
  • 1 cup American processed cheese (Eden brand), shredded
  • For the Latik:
  • 2 cups coconut cream

Instructions

  1. In a wide, heavy-bottomed pan over medium heat, combine grated ube, coconut milk, condensed milk, evaporated milk, butter, and sugar.
  2. Bring to a boil, stirring occasionally until sugar is dissolved, butter is melted, and ube is dispersed.
  3. Add ube extract, if using, and stir until mixture is evenly colored.
  4. Lower heat and continue to cook, stirring regularly, for about 30 to 40 minutes or until a soft, sticky dough forms.
  5. Add cheese and stir until melted. Continue to cook for another 10 to 15 minutes or until mixture is thick enough to cling to the back of the spoon and pulls slightly from the side of the pan.
  6. Lightly grease sides of a baking dish or llanera mold with coconut oil or melted butter. Spoon ube halaya into the prepared dish and using a buttered spatula, spread and flatten evenly. Allow to cool.
  7. To serve, invert halaya onto a serving plate. Lightly brush the top with coconut oil and garnish with latik, shredded cheese, toasted coconut flakes, or sweetened macapuno.
  8. To make Latik: In a pan over medium heat, add coconut cream and bring to a boil. Cook, stirring occasionally, until liquid starts to thicken. Lower heat and continue to cook, regularly stirring and scraping sides and bottom, until curds turn golden brown. Drain latik from the oil and store in separate containers until ready to use.

Maja Blanca

Filipino Maja Blanca Recipe

Maja Blanca is a Filipino dessert made from coconut milk, cornstarch, and sugar — often called Coconut Pudding. This luscious dessert is easy to make and the ingredients are very common. Unknown to many, the original Maja Blanca recipe does not include corn and condensed milk, but adding whole sweet kernel corn creates a more creamy texture and better taste. Maja Blanca is a rich, creamy coconut pudding that is sure to be a crowd favorite — great as a midday snack or dessert at Filipino fiestas, parties, and holiday tables.

Prep1 hr 50 mins (includes setting)
Cook10 mins
Total2 hrs
Servings8

Ingredients

  • 2 cans (13.5 ounces each) coconut milk
  • 1 can (14 ounces) cut corn, drained
  • 1/2 cup sugar
  • 1/4 cup cold water
  • 1/2 cup cornstarch
  • 1/4 cup desiccated coconut flakes

Instructions

  1. In a saucepan, combine coconut milk, corn, and sugar. Over medium heat, bring to a gentle boil, stirring occasionally until sugar is dissolved.
  2. In a small bowl, mix cornstarch and cold water until completely smooth. Slowly add to coconut milk, whisking vigorously to prevent lumps.
  3. Continue to cook, whisking regularly, for about 2 to 4 minutes or until mixture thickens to a smooth paste consistency.
  4. In a lightly greased 8 x 8 baking dish, transfer thickened coconut mixture. Using a spatula, smooth the surface.
  5. Allow pudding to cool for a few minutes. Cover and refrigerate for about 1 to 2 hours or until completely set.
  6. In an ungreased skillet over medium heat, toast shredded coconut, stirring frequently, for about 1 to 2 minutes or until golden brown. Remove from heat immediately.
  7. Sprinkle toasted coconut flakes on top and cut into squares to serve.
