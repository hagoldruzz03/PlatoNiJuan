Adobong Manok

Filipino Chicken Adobo Recipe

Chicken Adobo is an authentic Filipino dish and one of the most widely recognized Filipino foods worldwide. Not to be mistaken with Mexican adobo, this dish is uniquely prepared by stewing chicken in a savory combination of vinegar and soy sauce with garlic, bay leaves, and whole peppercorns. Filipino food historians note that the practice of cooking with vinegar predates Spanish colonization — our ancestors used it as a method of food preservation, making adobo one of the earliest preservation techniques in Philippine culinary history. The taste of juicy chicken in a succulent, umami-rich adobo sauce makes this dish a beloved comfort food for all ages, from kids to adults.

Prep20 mins
Cook45 mins
Total1 hr 5 mins
Servings6

Ingredients

  • 2 tablespoons vegetable oil
  • 1 (3 lb) chicken, cut into pieces
  • 1 large onion, quartered and sliced
  • 2 tablespoons minced garlic
  • ⅔ cup low sodium soy sauce
  • ⅓ cup white vinegar
  • 1 tablespoon garlic powder
  • 2 teaspoons black pepper
  • 1 bay leaf

Instructions

  1. Heat vegetable oil in a large skillet over medium-high heat. Cook chicken pieces until golden brown, 2–3 minutes per side. Transfer to a plate and set aside.
  2. Add onion and garlic to the skillet; cook until softened and brown, about 6 minutes.
  3. Pour in soy sauce and vinegar and season with garlic powder, black pepper, and bay leaf.
  4. Return chicken to pan, increase heat to high, and bring to a boil. Reduce heat to medium-low, cover, and simmer until chicken is tender, 35–40 minutes.

Afritadang Manok

Filipino Chicken Afritada Recipe

Chicken Afritada, or Afritadang Manok, is a classic Filipino chicken stew simmered in a rich tomato-based sauce with potatoes, carrots, and bell peppers. It is a simple yet deeply satisfying dish that can be prepared on regular weekdays, and is perfect for lunch or dinner served alongside warm white rice. The name "afritada" traces its roots to the Spanish word "freír" meaning to fry — a cooking method brought to the Philippines during the colonial era. Filipino cooks then developed the tradition of pan-frying vegetables and chicken before letting everything slow-stew together in savory aromatics and tomato sauce, giving the dish its signature depth of flavor. This improved version adds butter and green peas for extra richness.

Prep15 mins
Cook45 mins
Total1 hr
Servings6

Ingredients

  • 1 kg chicken, cut into serving pieces
  • 1 cup tomato sauce
  • 2 medium potatoes, cubed
  • 2 medium carrots, sliced
  • 1 red bell pepper, sliced
  • 1 green bell pepper, sliced
  • 1 onion, chopped
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 2 tablespoons fish sauce
  • Salt and pepper to taste

Instructions

  1. Sauté garlic and onion in oil until fragrant.
  2. Add chicken pieces and cook until lightly browned on all sides.
  3. Pour in tomato sauce and 1 cup water. Bring to a boil.
  4. Add potatoes and carrots. Simmer for 20–25 minutes until tender.
  5. Add bell peppers, season with fish sauce, salt and pepper. Cook 5 more minutes.

Chicken Barbeque

Filipino Chicken Barbeque Recipe

Good food often leaves quite the impression after even just the first taste — and Filipino Chicken Barbecue done right possesses that same unforgettable quality. The aroma, the blend of flavors, and the perfectly juicy texture all come together in a marinade of soy sauce, calamansi, sugar, and garlic. The barbecue tradition itself is believed to have ancient origins, tracing back to slow-cooking methods recorded in early Spanish journals describing Caribbean tribes who cooked meat over raised pits covered in hot coals. Today, this tradition lives on vibrantly in Filipino street food culture and backyard gatherings, where skewers of marinated chicken are grilled over charcoal and basted continuously for that signature smoky, caramelized crust.

Prep30 mins
Cook30 mins
Total1 hr
Servings6

Ingredients

  • 1 kg chicken, cut into pieces
  • ½ cup soy sauce
  • ¼ cup calamansi juice (or lemon juice)
  • ½ cup banana ketchup
  • ¼ cup sugar
  • 6 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 teaspoon black pepper
  • Salt to taste

Instructions

  1. Combine soy sauce, calamansi juice, banana ketchup, sugar, garlic, and pepper to make the marinade.
  2. Marinate chicken in the mixture for at least 1 hour (overnight preferred).
  3. Thread chicken onto skewers.
  4. Grill over medium-high heat, basting frequently with leftover marinade, until cooked through.

Chicken Caldereta

Filipino Chicken Caldereta Recipe

Chicken Caldereta is a hearty Filipino chicken stew simmered to perfect tenderness in a spicy, richly flavored tomato sauce with potatoes, bell peppers, and green olives. The dish takes its name from the Spanish word "caldera," meaning cauldron — a fitting name for a recipe that was introduced to the Philippines during the Spanish colonial period and adapted beautifully by Filipino cooks. What sets this version apart is the addition of liver spread, which lends a uniquely deep and savory richness to the sauce, and melted cheese that brings a creamy, velvety finish. Simple to make yet impressive enough for special occasions and family gatherings.

Prep20 mins
Cook50 mins
Total1 hr 10 mins
Servings6

Ingredients

  • 1 kg chicken, cut into pieces
  • 1 cup tomato sauce
  • 2 tablespoons liver spread
  • 2 potatoes, cubed
  • 2 carrots, sliced
  • 1 red bell pepper, sliced
  • ½ cup green olives
  • 1 teaspoon chili flakes
  • Salt and pepper to taste

Instructions

  1. Sauté garlic and onion. Add chicken and cook until browned.
  2. Add tomato sauce and liver spread; stir well.
  3. Add water or broth and bring to a boil. Add potatoes and carrots.
  4. Simmer 25 minutes. Add bell peppers, olives, and chili flakes.
  5. Season to taste. Simmer 5 more minutes and serve.

Chicken Curry

Filipino Chicken Curry Recipe

The Philippines is considered the melting pot of Asia — a nation shaped by centuries of trade, migration, and cultural exchange that deeply influenced its cuisine. This is how spices like curry found their way into Philippine cooking. This Filipino-style chicken curry is the local adaptation of the beloved dish, featuring moist, tender chicken in a rich, creamy sauce made with coconut milk. What makes it distinctly Pinoy is the inclusion of fish sauce, bell pepper, and celery — ingredients absent from traditional Indian versions. The result is a boldly aromatic, deeply satisfying curry that pairs beautifully with steamed white rice, and a dish that has earned a well-deserved place as a Filipino kitchen staple.

Prep15 mins
Cook40 mins
Total55 mins
Servings6

Ingredients

  • 1 kg chicken pieces
  • 2 tablespoons curry powder
  • 1 cup coconut milk
  • 2 potatoes, cubed
  • 1 red bell pepper, sliced
  • 1 stalk celery, sliced
  • 2 tablespoons fish sauce
  • 1 onion, sliced
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced

Instructions

  1. Sauté garlic and onion. Add curry powder and stir for 1 minute.
  2. Add chicken and cook until lightly browned.
  3. Add potatoes and 1 cup water; simmer 15 minutes.
  4. Pour in coconut milk, add celery and bell pepper.
  5. Season with fish sauce. Simmer until chicken is tender.

Sisig

The iconic Filipino dish of minced and grilled pig face — ears, jowls, and snout — combined with chicken liver, calamansi, onions, and chili. Originating in Pampanga and credited to the legendary "Sisig Queen" Aling Lucing, it shines both as a satisfying main dish with rice and as the ultimate beer food or pulutan.

1 hr 30 mins 6 servings
Pork

Bicol Express

A fiery and creamy pork belly stew from the Bicol region, cooked in coconut milk with shrimp paste (bagoong) and generous amounts of siling haba and Thai chili. The mild sweetness of coconut milk creates a spell-binding contrast with the bold chili heat — making Bicol Express one of the most popular and beloved dishes in all of Filipino cuisine. Rich in protein and packed with flavor, it is best enjoyed with plenty of steamed white rice.

1 hr 6 servings
Pork

Pork Bistek

A Filipino twist on the classic Bistek Tagalog, using tender bone-in pork chops braised in a savory marinade of soy sauce and calamansi juice, then finished with caramelized red onion rings. The citrusy tang and deep umami saltiness of the sauce make this dish irresistibly flavorful. Pork chops are more affordable and milder than beef, letting the seasoning shine. Quick to prepare and beginner-friendly, this is a wonderful everyday Filipino comfort meal.

45 mins 4 servings
Pork

Crispy Pata

A showstopping Filipino dish of whole pork leg — boiled until melt-in-your-mouth tender, then deep-fried to achieve a gloriously golden, crackling skin. It can be served as a hearty main dish with rice and atchara, or as a crowd-pleasing beer food. Best dipped in a spicy vinegar and soy sauce mixture. While cooking crispy pata requires patience and caution, the satisfying result makes it a true achievement in the kitchen.

2 hrs 6 servings
Pork

Dinuguan

A uniquely Filipino savory stew of pork belly simmered in a rich, dark gravy made from pig's blood, garlic, chili peppers, and vinegar. While unfamiliar to some, dinuguan is a deeply flavorful dish beloved across the Philippines. This version uses only pork belly — no innards — making it an approachable entry point for beginners. It is traditionally paired with soft, steamed puto for a classic and satisfying combination.

1 hr 6 servings
Pork

Lechon

The undisputed crown jewel of Filipino fiestas and celebrations — a whole suckling pig slow-roasted until the skin turns into an impossibly thin, shatteringly crisp shell encasing tender, juicy, herb-infused meat. This home adaptation features a mojo-style citrus and herb marinade of orange juice, lime, cilantro, oregano, and garlic, injected and rubbed all over the pig before a long roast. A dish that commands awe and feeds a crowd.

4–6 hrs 20+ servings
Pork

Pork Menudo

A beloved Filipino tomato-based pork stew with cubed pork shoulder, beef liver, potatoes, carrots, garbanzo beans, bell peppers, and sweet raisins. A staple at Philippine town fiestas and family gatherings, Pork Menudo has countless regional variations — from versions with hotdog to those with sun-dried raisins. Rich, hearty, and deeply satisfying, this dish is a true celebration of Filipino home cooking at its best.

1 hr 6 servings
Pork

Nilagang Baboy

A soul-warming Filipino pork soup made by slowly boiling pork belly in a clear broth with onions, peppercorns, and vegetables like calabasa and pechay. Nilaga is the ultimate rainy-day comfort food — simple in preparation yet deeply nourishing. It is typically served hot with fish sauce and chili on the side, and best enjoyed with a generous bowl of steamed white rice.

Pork

Paksiw na Pata

Pork hocks slow-braised in a combination of vinegar, soy sauce, garlic, bay leaves, and peppercorns until the meat is fall-off-the-bone tender and the sauce is rich and thick. Banana blossoms and brown sugar add a subtle sweetness that balances the tang of the paksiw braise. This dish closely resembles Pork Adobo in its flavor profile but the extended braising produces an even more tender, deeply flavored result.

2 hrs 6 servings
Pork

Pork Sinigang

The iconic Filipino sour soup — pork spare ribs simmered in a tangy tamarind broth with tomatoes, onion, taro, radish, eggplant, long beans, okra, and leafy greens. Sinigang is one of the most popular dishes in Filipino cuisine, even rated the world's best vegetable soup by TasteAtlas in 2021. The hot, sour broth delivers unmatched comfort in any weather, and the medley of vegetables makes every bowl nutritious and deeply satisfying.

Fish & Seafood Recipes

Fish

Adobong Isda

The classic Filipino adobo method applied to fresh fish — most commonly galunggong (round scad) — cooked in a savory combination of soy sauce, vinegar, garlic, bay leaves, and peppercorns. Fish adobo is easy to prepare, wonderfully flavorful, and pairs perfectly with steamed white rice. Any leftovers can be fried the next day for an equally delicious version. A quintessential example of how Filipino cooking transforms simple ingredients into something extraordinary.

30 mins 4 servings
Fish

Adobong Hito

A traditional Filipino adobo variation using catfish (hito) — pan-fried until crisp and golden, then simmered in a tangy vinegar and soy sauce braising liquid with onion, ginger, tomatoes, star anise, and bay leaves. Ginger is key here, neutralizing the fish's strong aroma while adding a warm, aromatic depth. Not only delicious and nutritious, but also easy to make and budget-friendly — best enjoyed with steamed rice.

40 mins 4 servings
Fish

Bangsilog

One of the most popular Filipino breakfast combinations — Bang (bangus/milkfish), Si (sinangag/garlic fried rice), and Log (itlog/fried egg). The bangus is marinated overnight in vinegar, garlic, and pepper, then fried to a beautiful golden-brown crispness. Part of the beloved "silog" family of Filipino breakfast meals, Bangsilog is hearty, flavorful, and a perfect way to start the day.

25 mins 2 servings
Fish

Totsong Bangus

A classic Filipino dish of fried milkfish stewed in a fresh tomato and onion sauce enriched with tahure (fermented bean cakes) and tausi (fermented black beans). The combination of these distinctly umami fermented ingredients gives Totsong Bangus its unique, deeply savory character — most likely a dish of Chinese culinary influence. A wonderfully tasty way to enjoy bangus for lunch or dinner paired with steamed rice.

35 mins 4 servings
Fish

Fried Daing

Also known as Sarciadong Daing or Sarciadong Buwad, this dish features salted dried fish (daing) quickly braised in a savory tomato-based sauce with garlic and onion until the liquid reduces and the flavors are absorbed. The dried fish is first soaked to remove excess salt, then cooked in a light, aromatic sauce. An easy, budget-friendly dish that can be prepared in under 30 minutes — best served with steamed white rice.

15 mins 2 servings
Fish

Kinunot

A prized dish from the Bicol region made with flaked stingray (pagi) simmered in spiced coconut cream with malunggay (moringa) leaves and chili peppers. "Kinunot" means flaked in Bicolano, referring to the technique of pulling the stingray meat from its cartilage. True to Bicol's culinary identity, this dish is built on coconut, chili, and fresh seafood — resulting in a creamy, mildly spicy, and wonderfully aromatic dish that is deeply rooted in regional tradition.

50 mins 4 servings
Fish

Lamayo

A popular Filipino fish preparation where fresh tilapia is split, marinated in a brine of vinegar, salt, sugar, garlic, pepper, and bay leaf, then partially sun-dried before frying. Lamayo sits between fresh fish and fully dried daing — retaining moisture while developing a concentrated, tangy-savory flavor. Commonly found in wet markets across the Philippines, it is a favorite breakfast or lunch dish served with garlic rice and a side of fresh tomatoes.

30 mins 4 servings
Fish

Pinangat na Isda

A simple and comforting Filipino fish dish where fish is simmered together with bilimbi (kamias), tomatoes, and peppercorns in a clear, naturally sour broth. One of the easiest Filipino recipes to prepare — no sautéing required, just combine and cook — it delivers a light, clean, and refreshing flavor that lets the natural taste of the fish shine. Pinangat na Isda is a nostalgic home-cooked dish best enjoyed with steamed white rice.

30 mins 4 servings
Fish

Sinanglay

A beautiful and aromatic Bicolano dish where whole tilapia is stuffed with tomatoes, shallots, and ginger, then wrapped in pechay (bok choy) leaves and tied with pandan leaves before being gently simmered in coconut milk with chili peppers. The pandan imparts a lovely fragrance and keeps the stuffing secure during cooking. Sinanglay is a type of ginataan and a wonderful showcase of Bicol's rich coconut-based culinary heritage.

45 mins 4 servings
Fish

Fish Sinigang

Sinigang na Bangus is a classic Filipino sour soup made with milkfish simmered in a bright tamarind broth with tomatoes, onion, radish, okra, kangkong, and chili peppers. Lighter and more delicate than pork sinigang, this version comes together in under 30 minutes with no sautéing required — just simmer and serve. It is a nourishing, refreshing dish that is perfect for any season, and especially soothing on a cool or rainy day.

Vegetable Recipes

Vegetable

Adobong Sitaw

Yard-long string beans (sitaw) cooked in the classic Filipino adobo style — sautéed with garlic and onion, then braised in vinegar and soy sauce until perfectly tender-crisp. Adobong Sitaw proves that the beloved adobo method works beautifully on vegetables too, bringing out the natural flavor of the beans in a savory, tangy sauce. Optional pork belly strips add a satisfying meaty depth, but it can easily be prepared as a fully vegetarian dish.

25 mins 4 servings
Vegetable

Ampalaya Salad

A refreshing and nutritious Filipino salad of thinly sliced bitter melon (ampalaya), onion, and tomatoes dressed in a light olive oil and vinegar vinaigrette. The salt-rubbing and soaking technique reduces the bitter melon's sharpness before serving, making it pleasantly tangy rather than overwhelming. Ampalaya is rich in vitamin C and known for its anti-diabetes properties. This salad makes an excellent side dish alongside fried fish or grilled seafood.

15 mins 4 servings
Vegetable

Bulanglang

Often called the healthiest Filipino soup, Bulanglang is a nourishing medley of fresh vegetables — including squash, taro, loofah, winged beans, and saluyot — simmered together in a light fish or shrimp broth. Its preparation varies across regions, but the essence is always a simple, mild, vegetable-forward dish that gets its flavor purely from the produce and a touch of bagoong (shrimp paste). Best paired with grilled or fried fish.

35 mins 4 servings
Vegetable

Chop Seuy

Filipino-style Chop Suey is a vibrant stir-fry of parboiled mixed vegetables — broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage, carrots, and bell peppers — tossed with chicken, chicken liver, and quail eggs in a savory oyster sauce broth. Although its exact origins are debated, this dish arrived in the Philippines through Chinese culinary influence and has since become a beloved Filipino party and everyday dish. Blanching the vegetables first keeps them bright, crisp, and colorful.

30 mins 4 servings
Vegetable

Ginataang Langka

Unripe jackfruit (langka) treated as a vegetable and slowly simmered in coconut milk with dried fish (danggit), garlic, onion, and chili until the sauce thickens into a rich, creamy coconut gravy. Although jackfruit is a fruit, its unripe form has the firm texture and neutral flavor of a vegetable — perfect for absorbing the bold flavors of this coconut-based dish. This is a specialty of the Romblon province and a wonderfully satisfying Filipino comfort food.

45 mins 4 servings
Vegetable

Ginisang Ampalaya

Sautéed bitter melon (ampalaya) with shrimp, tomatoes, garlic, onion, and beaten eggs — a well-balanced dish that tempers the vegetable's natural bitterness into a pleasantly savory flavor. Ampalaya is packed with vitamin C and contains an insulin-like peptide that makes it popular for managing blood sugar. Soaking the sliced ampalaya in cold salted water before cooking is the key technique for reducing its bitterness and making this nutritious dish genuinely delicious.

20 mins 3 servings
Vegetable

Laing

A celebrated dish from the Bicol Region — shredded dried taro leaves slow-cooked in rich coconut milk with pork belly, shrimp paste, lemongrass, and Thai chili peppers until the leaves are silky-soft and the sauce has thickened into a deeply aromatic, creamy coconut gravy. The key is never stirring the leaves in the first 15–20 minutes, allowing them to absorb the liquid gradually. Laing is a beloved Filipino comfort dish often served as a vegetable side alongside grilled meat or fried fish.

1 hr 6 servings
Vegetable

Monggo Guisado

Sautéed mung beans (mongo) slowly simmered with pork strips, ampalaya, and malunggay leaves in a savory tomato-garlic broth. Soaking the dried mung beans overnight softens them and dramatically reduces cooking time. A Friday tradition in many Filipino households, Monggo Guisado is one of the most affordable yet protein-rich dishes in Filipino cuisine. Best topped with crispy chicharon and served alongside any fried dish.

45 mins 6 servings
Vegetable

Pinakbet

Named after the Ilocano word "pinakebbet" meaning shriveled or reduced, Pinakbet is a Filipino mixed vegetable stew of squash, okra, eggplant, bitter melon, and long beans cooked in a savory shrimp paste (bagoong alamang) sauce with pork belly. As the vegetables slowly cook down and absorb the salty, umami-rich bagoong, they shrink and intensify in flavor — resulting in a deeply satisfying dish that pairs beautifully with fried fish and steamed rice.

35 mins 4 servings
Vegetable

Tortang Talong

A beloved Filipino eggplant omelette made by flame-roasting whole eggplants until charred and tender, then peeling, flattening, dipping in seasoned beaten egg, and pan-frying until golden brown. Quick, easy, and endlessly versatile, Tortang Talong is a popular breakfast and lunch staple in Filipino homes. Eggplant is rich in vitamins C, K, and B6, making this simple dish not just delicious but genuinely nutritious. A common variation, rellenong talong, stuffs the eggplant with seasoned ground meat.

25 mins 3 servings

Dessert Recipes

Dessert

Fruit Salad

A beloved Filipino party and holiday dessert staple — a medley of fresh seasonal fruits like strawberries, mango, kiwi, grapes, and apples tossed in a honey-citrus dressing and chilled until ready to serve. Light, refreshing, and naturally sweet, Filipino Fruit Salad requires no cooking and comes together in minutes. It is highly recommended for beginner cooks and is a guaranteed crowd-pleaser at any gathering or celebration.

20 mins 8 servings
Dessert

Ginataang Bilo-bilo

A perfectly sweet and creamy Filipino dessert soup of chewy handmade glutinous rice balls (bilo-bilo), mini sago pearls, and sweetened ripe jackfruit simmered in rich coconut milk and sugar. Warm, hearty, and deeply comforting, Ginataang Bilo-bilo is a popular Filipino merienda (afternoon snack) or dessert. It is considered a simplified version of the more elaborate Ginataang Halo-halo, and can be served hot or at room temperature.

45 mins 6 servings
Dessert

Halo-halo

The ultimate Filipino summer treat — a towering glass of finely shaved ice layered with sweetened beans, jellies, tropical fruits, nata de coco, ube halaya, leche flan, and topped with a generous scoop of ice cream and evaporated milk. "Halo-halo" literally means "mix-mix," and the dish is believed to have evolved from the Japanese kakigori tradition brought to the Philippines by early settlers. Endlessly customizable and refreshingly delicious, it is a true Filipino dessert icon.

20 mins 2 servings
Dessert

Leche Flan

The ultimate Filipino custard dessert — a silky smooth, rich caramel-topped custard made with egg yolks, sweetened condensed milk, and evaporated milk, steamed or baked in oval llanera molds. Unlike the French crème caramel, Filipino Leche Flan uses no cream, relying instead on condensed milk for its signature dense sweetness and velvety texture. A staple at Filipino fiestas, birthday parties, and holiday celebrations, and a key topping in the beloved halo-halo.

1 hr 6 servings
Dessert

Mais Con Yelo

A brilliantly simple yet refreshing Filipino cold dessert — sweet whole kernel corn layered with crushed ice, drizzled with condensed and evaporated milk, topped with crushed cornflakes, and optionally crowned with a scoop of vanilla or sweet corn ice cream. Mais con Yelo (maíz con hielo in Spanish) celebrates the creative Filipino use of corn in sweet preparations, and is one of the most beloved summer snacks across the Philippines.

10 mins 2 servings
Dessert

Puto Bumbong

An iconic Filipino Christmas delicacy — ground purple glutinous rice steamed inside narrow bamboo tubes (bumbong), then plated on a banana leaf and topped with margarine, freshly grated coconut, and muscovado sugar. Puto Bumbong is synonymous with the Filipino Christmas tradition of Simbang Gabi (dawn masses), where vendors line the church courtyard offering this fragrant, earthy purple rice cake to churchgoers in the early morning chill.

1 hr 8 servings
Dessert

Cheesy Puto

Soft, fluffy Filipino steamed rice cakes with pockets of melted cheddar cheese in every bite. Made from flour, sugar, eggs, evaporated milk, and vanilla, Cheese Puto is a classic merienda or breakfast treat that can be made from scratch in under 40 minutes. The combination of the mildly sweet cake and the savory, salty cheese makes it irresistible. Puto is also a traditional accompaniment to Dinuguan for a famously satisfying Filipino flavor pairing.

45 mins 12 pieces
Dessert

Taho

One of the most nostalgic Filipino street foods — warm, silky-smooth tofu served in individual cups with chewy sago pearls and arnibal, a sweet brown sugar syrup infused with pandan. The familiar call of "Tahoooo!" from the magtataho (taho vendor) carrying his large aluminum buckets through the streets is a cherished childhood memory for millions of Filipinos. This homemade version recreates that street-food warmth using soft silken tofu and a simple pandan-flavored syrup.

20 mins 4 servings
Dessert

Ube Halaya

A deeply indulgent Filipino purple yam jam made by slow-cooking grated ube with coconut milk, condensed milk, evaporated milk, butter, sugar, and melted cheese until it becomes a thick, velvety, intensely purple confection. Ube Halaya (also called ube jam or ube haleya) is beloved both as a standalone dessert and as an ingredient in halo-halo, puto, and cakes. Garnished with golden latik (toasted coconut curds) and shredded cheese, it is a true Filipino culinary treasure.

1 hr 8 servings
Dessert

Maja Blanca

A rich and creamy Filipino coconut pudding made from coconut milk, cornstarch, whole sweet corn kernels, and sugar — set into a smooth, sliceable dessert and finished with golden toasted coconut flakes on top. Often called "Coconut Pudding," Maja Blanca is a popular fixture at Filipino fiestas, parties, and holiday tables. The original recipe does not use corn or condensed milk, but these additions create a more luscious, flavorful version that Filipinos have come to love.

40 mins 8 servings
